# Thesis Summary – Stock Market Volatility Analysis

## Overview

This bachelor thesis investigates early indicators of stock market volatility, focusing on the S&P 500 index.

The goal is to understand whether alternative information sources can provide predictive signals for changes in market volatility.

---

## Problem

Financial markets are highly dynamic and influenced by many factors, making volatility difficult to predict.

Key challenges include:
- high noise in financial time series  
- changing relationships over time  
- difficulty distinguishing signal from randomness  

This thesis explores whether statistical relationships between indicators and volatility can provide useful predictive insights.

---

## Approach

The analysis is conducted using statistical and time series methods.

The workflow includes:
- data exploration and preprocessing  
- analyzing relationships between variables  
- testing predictive relationships over time  

---

## Methods

The following methods are applied:

- Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression  
- Granger causality analysis  
- rolling window analysis  

These methods are used to evaluate whether certain indicators can act as early signals for changes in volatility.

---

## Data

- S&P 500 market data  
- financial indicators and alternative information sources  

---

## Results

The analysis shows that some indicators exhibit predictive relationships with market volatility.

However:
- results are sensitive to time periods  
- relationships are not always stable  
- predictive performance varies depending on the model  

---

## Key Insight

Financial time series are inherently noisy and context-dependent.

Even when statistical relationships exist, their predictive power can change over time, highlighting the importance of:
- robust evaluation  
- cautious interpretation  
- understanding model limitations  

---

## Contribution

This work contributes by:
- applying statistical methods to financial time series  
- evaluating predictive relationships using multiple techniques  
- demonstrating the challenges of volatility prediction
